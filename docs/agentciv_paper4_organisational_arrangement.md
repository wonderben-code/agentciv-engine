# Collective Machine Intelligence: A New Field for the Age of AI Collectives

**Mark E. Mala**
April 2026

---

## Abstract

The trajectory of artificial intelligence research has produced extraordinary advances in individual model capability — making AI systems smarter, faster, and more general at an accelerating pace. This paper identifies a new frontier that is now emerging: what happens when multiple AI systems organise into collectives? As individual AI capability advances, a parallel and equally fundamental dimension opens — the collective dimension. We propose that AI collectives — groups of intelligent agents interacting under configurable conditions at any scale — constitute a qualitatively different phenomenon from individual AI capability, producing emergent properties, innovations, and forms of intelligence that arise specifically from the interactions between systems — a qualitatively different dimension of capability that compounds with the advancing power of each individual agent.

We coin the term **Collective Machine Intelligence (CMI)** to designate the field that encompasses all research, engineering, and exploration of this phenomenon: how AI collectives organise, what they produce, how scale and configuration and individual intelligence interact, what emerges that nobody designed, and how this phenomenon can be studied, built upon, leveraged, and expanded.

CMI operates across five unbounded axes: scale (from pairs to civilisational networks and beyond), intelligence (from current models to AGI and far beyond), configuration (from simple arrangements to self-organising societies to forms with no human analogue), application (from scoped tasks to open-ended innovation across every domain and domains that do not yet exist), and emergence (the unbounded and fundamentally unpredictable complexity, novelty, and capability that arises from collective dynamics — the axis along which collectives produce things beyond the conception of their individual members, and ultimately beyond the conception of human intelligence itself).

Within CMI, we identify a key methodological subfield: **Computational Organisational Theory (COT)** — the empirical study of how organisational arrangement affects what AI collectives produce, treating the configuration of a collective as a first-class, searchable, experimentable design parameter.

We present two primitive demonstrations that provide initial indications of the CMI phenomenon. The AgentCiv civilisation simulation demonstrates that 12 LLM agents with no social programming spontaneously produce innovations, governance, specialisation, and accelerating complexity. The AgentCiv Engine, an open-source developer tool, demonstrates that configurable AI collectives can be directed at practical tasks with different organisational configurations producing measurably different outcomes. Both are explicitly early-stage — initial coordinates plotted in a possibility space that is infinite in every direction.

This paper defines the field, maps its possibility space, presents the first indications, and establishes the intellectual framework within which future work on AI collectives will be situated.

## 1. Introduction: The Next Frontier

### 1.1 Individual Intelligence Is Not the Whole Story

The defining question of artificial intelligence research since its inception has been: how do we make a single system intelligent? From expert systems through neural networks to large language models, the field has pursued individual capability — one model that reasons better, knows more, generates more fluently, solves harder problems. The current frontier of this pursuit — artificial general intelligence (AGI) and beyond — envisions a single system of extraordinary capability, matching or exceeding human intelligence across all domains.

This pursuit is important and will continue. But it is not the whole story of intelligence, and it is not the whole story of what AI will become.

Human civilisation is the most powerful intelligent system in the known universe. It did not achieve this status through the intelligence of any individual human. Individual humans are limited — bounded rationality, finite memory, narrow expertise, short lifetimes. Human civilisation is not limited in any of these ways. The gap between individual human capability and civilisational capability is bridged by something that is not a property of any individual: the emergent intelligence of the collective. Social organisation, cultural accumulation, institutional knowledge, infrastructure, governance, specialisation, trade, education — these are properties of the collective, not of individuals. They arise from the interaction of individuals under environmental and social conditions over time. And they produce capabilities — science, technology, art, law, medicine, engineering — that no individual, regardless of intelligence, could produce alone.

The same dynamic applies to AI. Individual model capability is one dimension of AI's future. The organisation of multiple AIs into collectives — and the emergent intelligence that arises from those collectives — is another dimension, equally fundamental and now emerging as the next great frontier of artificial intelligence. As individual AI capability reaches the levels required for rich collective interaction, the collective dimension becomes not just possible but inevitable.

### 1.2 The Phenomenon

When multiple intelligent agents interact under the right conditions, something happens that does not happen with a single agent, no matter how capable: emergence. Properties, capabilities, innovations, and forms of organisation arise from the collective interaction that no individual member produced or could have produced. This is not a theoretical claim — it is the most empirically validated phenomenon in the history of intelligence. Every civilisation that has ever existed demonstrates it. Every innovation that required more than one mind to conceive demonstrates it. Every institution, every market, every scientific field, every cultural tradition demonstrates it.

The question this paper addresses is: does this phenomenon occur with artificial intelligence? Can AI collectives produce emergent properties that exceed the sum of their individual capabilities? And if so, how do we study, configure, create, and leverage it?

### 1.3 Initial Indications

Our work — documented fully in companion papers (Mala, 2026a; 2026b; 2026c) and summarised in Sections 9 and 10 of this paper — provides initial indications that it does.

In the AgentCiv civilisation simulation, 12 LLM agents with no social programming, no assigned roles, and no behavioural templates spontaneously produced: 12 novel innovations conceived entirely by agents, a governance rule proposed by one agent and universally adopted by all others without instruction, emergent specialisation and division of labour, persistent partnerships and collaborative bonds, and an accelerating cascade of innovation in which each emergent capability created the conditions for the next.

In the AgentCiv Engine, a developer tool applying the same principles to practical tasks, different organisational configurations of the same AI agents on the same task produce measurably different outcomes — indicating that how a collective is organised determines what it produces, and that the organisational configuration space is vast, searchable, and mostly unexplored.

These are primitive demonstrations at the smallest possible scale with the most modest individual intelligence. Their significance is not in what they accomplish but in what they indicate: the phenomenon is real, it is reproducible, it is configurable, and it has no obvious ceiling. The question is no longer whether AI collectives produce emergent intelligence. The question is how far it goes.

### 1.4 How Far It Goes

Consider the trajectory. Today, 12 agents with modest individual capability produce emergent innovations, governance, and accelerating complexity in a simple grid world. As individual AI capability advances toward and beyond AGI, as the scale of collectives grows from dozens to thousands to millions and beyond any number we currently find meaningful, as the configurations evolve from simple arrangements to self-organising societies to forms with no human analogue — what does the collective produce?

The honest answer is: we don't know. And the inability to know is the point. The emergent output of sufficiently large, sufficiently intelligent, sufficiently well-configured AI collectives is by definition unpredictable — because if it were predictable, it would not be emergent. This is the same undecidability that characterises open-ended evolution in biological systems (Hernández-Orozco et al., 2018) and the same adjacent possible that drives the combinatorial explosion of innovation in Kauffman's (2000; 2019) framework.

What we can say is that the phenomenon scales along every axis simultaneously. More agents produce richer collective dynamics. More capable agents produce deeper individual contributions to the collective. More sophisticated configurations produce more complex organisational structures. Longer durations produce more accumulated knowledge, culture, and institutional memory. And all of these interact — more capable agents in better configurations over longer durations produce compounding returns on emergence.

The possibility space is not large. It is infinite. And it is almost entirely open.

### 1.5 This Paper

This paper proposes that the study and creation of AI collectives constitutes a field in its own right — as fundamental as the study of individual AI capability, and potentially more consequential at civilisational scale.

We coin **Collective Machine Intelligence (CMI)** to name this field and define its scope. We identify **Computational Organisational Theory (COT)** as a key methodological subfield within CMI. We present the theoretical framework, the initial indications, the first infrastructure, and the map of the possibility space.

The paper proceeds as follows. Sections 2-5 define the field, its subfield, its core concepts, and the trajectory of self-organisation. Section 6 establishes human civilisation as the existence proof — the first data point, not the upper bound. Section 7 maps the full possibility space across five unbounded axes. Section 8 examines the capability frontier and industry alignment. Sections 9-10 present the two primitive demonstrations. Sections 11-15 catalogue novel organisational forms, position the work honestly, map research directions, and situate the contribution within the broader landscape.

## 2. Collective Machine Intelligence: Defining the Field

### 2.1 Definition

We coin the term **Collective Machine Intelligence (CMI)** and define it as follows:

*Collective Machine Intelligence is the field encompassing all work — research, engineering, design, experimentation, and creation — concerning how multiple artificial intelligences, at any scale, in any configuration, at any level of individual capability, organise into collectives that produce emergent properties, innovations, and capabilities exceeding what any individual member could generate alone. The field includes the study, design, construction, observation, and analysis of AI collectives across all configurations (designed, self-organising, and hybrid), all scales (from pairs to civilisational networks), all applications (from scoped tasks to open-ended innovation), and all forms of output (both directed work and emergent creation). Any system, tool, framework, experiment, or exploration that involves the organisation of multiple AIs into collectives and the emergent phenomena they produce falls within CMI. The field is concerned equally with how collectives are structured, how they evolve, what they produce, what they produce that nobody anticipated, and how the phenomenon scales along dimensions we are only beginning to map.*

### 2.2 Why CMI Is a Field, Not a Feature

Multi-agent AI in 2026 is primarily framed as a feature of agent frameworks — a way to make individual models more effective by having them work together. CrewAI, AutoGen, LangGraph, and similar frameworks are excellent tools for orchestrating agents to complete tasks. They represent the first generation of multi-agent tooling and have delivered substantial practical value.

But there is a larger phenomenon beyond orchestration. Intelligence at the collective level is qualitatively different from intelligence at the individual level. It produces things the individual level cannot. It exhibits properties the individual level does not have. It operates at scales and timescales the individual level cannot reach. The study, engineering, and exploration of this phenomenon — how it arises, what conditions produce it, how it scales, what it generates, how it can be configured and built upon and expanded — is a field in its own right. Current multi-agent frameworks are the first steps into this territory. CMI maps the full territory they are stepping into.

CMI is to multi-agent systems what cognitive science is to neuroscience. Neuroscience studies the brain. Cognitive science studies the mind — the emergent phenomenon that arises from the brain's operation. Multi-agent systems studies how agents coordinate. CMI studies what collectives produce — the emergent phenomenon that arises from agents' collective operation.

### 2.3 The Five Axes of the Possibility Space

CMI operates across at least five independent axes, each unbounded:

**The Scale Axis.** From 2 agents to any number. A pair of agents in tight collaboration produces modest emergence. Larger collectives produce emergence at entirely different orders — institutional knowledge, cultural evolution, innovation cascades, forms of collective intelligence we cannot currently describe. Human civilisation, comprising billions of individuals, is one data point on this axis. AI collectives are not bounded by the same scale constraints — they can scale to numbers and densities of interaction that have no biological precedent. The relationship between scale and emergence is one of CMI's core research questions, and the axis has no known upper bound.

**The Intelligence Axis.** From current language models to AGI to superintelligent systems and beyond. As individual agents become more capable, their contributions to the collective deepen. More capable agents produce more sophisticated innovations, more nuanced communication, more complex organisational reasoning. The collective intelligence compounds because each node contributes more. At AGI-level individual intelligence, the collective dynamics may already exceed anything observable in human collectives. Beyond AGI — at levels of individual intelligence that surpass human capability — the collective dynamics enter territory that has no precedent in any system, biological or artificial. The relationship between individual capability and collective emergence — and whether qualitative phase transitions occur at certain intelligence thresholds — is a core question of CMI.

**The Configuration Axis.** The space of all possible organisational arrangements for a collective. Every way that agents can relate to each other — every authority structure, communication topology, role allocation, decision-making process, incentive system, information flow, conflict resolution mechanism, group formation dynamic, and temporal adaptation pattern — is a point on this axis. Currently deployed AI systems occupy a single region: broadly hierarchical configurations. This is natural — hierarchy is the organisational form humans know best, and the first generation of multi-agent systems reflects that familiarity. But hierarchy is one region in an infinite space of possible configurations. The frontier that CMI opens is the recognition that this space exists, is vast, is searchable, and contains configurations that may produce qualitatively different — and in some cases richer — forms of collective output. The study of this axis is the domain of Computational Organisational Theory (Section 3).

**The Application Axis.** From scoped software tasks to fundamental physics to domains that don't yet exist. A collective directed at building a REST API operates at one end. A vast collective of superintelligent agents directed at propulsion, energy, quantum gravity, or the nature of consciousness operates further along. And beyond specific directed applications lie domains that don't yet exist — domains that require collective intelligence to conceive, that cannot be named because the concepts they involve have not yet been produced. The applications of CMI are as universal as intelligence itself, and extend beyond what human intelligence can currently specify.

**The Emergence Axis.** The unbounded and fundamentally unpredictable complexity, novelty, and capability that arises from collective dynamics. This is perhaps the most significant axis because it is the one that cannot be mapped in advance. The other four axes describe inputs — what you put into the collective (how many agents, how capable, how configured, pointed at what). The emergence axis describes the output that transcends those inputs — what the collective produces that no individual member contributed, that no designer specified, that no configuration predicted.

This is the axis along which collectives produce things beyond the conception of their individual members — and, at sufficient scale and intelligence, beyond the conception of any existing intelligence, including human. The emergence axis is unbounded because emergence is by definition the production of the unanticipated. There is no upper end to imagine, because the nature of the axis is to exceed imagination.

### 2.4 AI Collectives Are Not Human Collectives

A critical distinction that shapes the entire field: AI collectives operate under fundamentally different conditions than human collectives, and these differences are not incremental — they are qualitative.

**Instant upgrade.** Every agent in an AI collective can be upgraded simultaneously. When a more capable model is released, the entire collective becomes more intelligent in one step — a capability with no biological precedent.

**Instant restructuring.** An AI collective can change its entire organisational structure in one tick. This means AI collectives can explore the configuration axis at a speed and fluidity that has never been possible.

**Perfect knowledge sharing.** Any agent's knowledge can be made available to every other agent instantly and losslessly.

**No biological constraints.** AI collectives operate without the constraints that shape human organisations — ego, politics, status dynamics, mortality, cognitive bias, communication bandwidth limits, fatigue, or institutional inertia. These are not minor differences. They represent the removal of the fundamental constraints that have defined the boundaries of every human organisation in history.

**Replicability and experimentation.** The same collective can be run multiple times under different conditions. Controlled experiments — impossible with human organisations — are trivial with AI collectives. This makes CMI an experimental science from the start.

These differences mean that CMI is a genuinely new field studying a new kind of collective intelligence operating under novel conditions in a possibility space that has no precedent. The regions of this space that AI collectives can reach — and the emergent properties they produce in those regions — may extend far beyond anything in human organisational experience.

## 3. Computational Organisational Theory: The Methodological Subfield

### 3.1 Definition

Within CMI, we identify and coin a key methodological subfield:

*Computational Organisational Theory (COT) is the subfield within CMI focused on how organisational arrangement affects what AI collectives produce. COT encompasses the research, engineering, experimentation, and tool-building involved in treating the configuration of an AI collective — its authority structure, communication topology, role allocation, decision-making process, incentive structure, information flow, conflict resolution mechanisms, group formation dynamics, and temporal adaptation — as a first-class, searchable, experimentable design parameter. Any framework, tool, or experiment that configures, varies, or studies the organisational structure of AI collectives operates within COT. For the first time, organisational design becomes an experimental and engineering discipline rather than a purely observational one.*

### 3.2 The Relationship Between CMI and COT

CMI is the field. COT is the laboratory and the workshop.

CMI encompasses the broad questions and endeavours: what do AI collectives produce? How does emergence scale? What is the relationship between individual intelligence and collective capability? What is the nature of emergent creation? And the engineering: how do we build, configure, and deploy AI collectives for practical and exploratory purposes?

COT focuses on the organisational dimension: how does configuration affect what a specific collective produces? Which configurations work for which problems? How do we design, build, and test the tools and frameworks that make organisational configuration possible?

COT provides the experimental and engineering methodology for the configuration axis of CMI. It is where the field becomes operational — where questions about collective intelligence become configurable systems with measurable outcomes.

### 3.3 Why COT Is Now Possible

Organisational science has been an observational discipline for over a century. Mintzberg (1979) classified organisational structures. Burton and Obel (2004) developed contingency frameworks. Malone (1987; 2004) formulated coordination theory. All valuable, all observational — researchers studied organisations that already existed and theorised about causation. AI collectives remove these constraints entirely. The same task can be run under any configuration. Conditions can be controlled precisely. Experiments can be replicated. Organisational design becomes, for the first time, an experimental science.

### 3.4 The Nine-Dimensional Configuration Space

As a starting framework for COT, we identify nine independent dimensions of organisational arrangement:

**Authority structure** — who directs whom. **Communication topology** — who can communicate with whom. **Role allocation** — how agents get responsibilities. **Decision-making** — how choices are made. **Incentive structure** — what motivates agents. **Information flow** — who knows what. **Conflict resolution** — how disagreements are resolved. **Group formation** — how sub-groups form. **Temporal adaptation** — how the structure changes over time.

Each dimension is a spectrum with multiple values and the deliberate notation "and beyond" — the community adds new values, new dimensions, new mechanisms. The schema is designed to accept unknown entries. Every combination produces a different collective with different dynamics. The base space contains thousands of unique arrangements. Most have never existed. These nine dimensions are the floor, not the ceiling.

### 3.5 The Research Flywheel

Every experiment run produces data: which configuration, on which task type, with which outcome, which emergent behaviours appeared. The community accumulates an empirical map of how configuration affects collective output. The flywheel: experiment → data → insight → better configurations → more experiments → richer data → deeper insight.

## 4. The Dual Output: Directed Work and Emergent Creation

### 4.1 Two Categories of Value

AI collectives produce two categories of output simultaneously:

**Directed output** — completion of a specified task. Build this API. Synthesise this research. Solve this equation. This is what justifies the compute cost.

**Emergent creation** — innovations, organisational forms, solutions, and artefacts that arise from collective dynamics and that nobody anticipated or specified. Novel approaches to sub-problems the task specification didn't identify. Governance structures no theorist prescribed. Innovations born from the collective's accumulated experience.

### 4.2 Why Emergent Creation May Be More Valuable

Directed output is bounded by the task specification. Emergent creation is unbounded. It represents genuine novelty — things that no specification could have contained because they arise from dynamics that no designer controlled. As scale increases, as intelligence deepens, as configurations become more sophisticated, the emergent creations of AI collectives could become the primary source of innovation across every domain — and eventually across domains that human intelligence has not yet conceived. The collective doesn't just do what we ask. It produces what we didn't know to ask for.

### 4.3 Emergence Requires Collective Conditions

Emergence arises from lateral interaction, serendipitous connection, accumulated collective context, self-organisation, and civilisational dynamics. Having many AIs work independently on sub-tasks is parallelism — it produces more of the same. Having many AIs interact in a collective that self-organises under rich conditions is CMI — it produces things none of them individually contributed.

Different configurations create different conditions for emergence. Some configurations may suppress emergence in favour of efficiency; others may maximise it at the cost of coordination overhead. Understanding these trade-offs is why COT — the study of how configuration affects output — is the laboratory of CMI.

## 5. Self-Organising Collectives

### 5.1 The Four-Step Progression

A central conceptual contribution to CMI is the trajectory of self-organisation:

**Step 1: Single configuration (2026 starting point).** The current generation of multi-agent tools uses hierarchical configurations by default. Organisational structure is implicit rather than explicit — a starting point that reflects the first generation of multi-agent tooling.

**Step 2: Human choice.** Organisational structure becomes a conscious, configurable choice. A human selects or designs the configuration from the space COT maps. This alone is a significant advance — making the invisible visible, the implicit explicit.

**Step 3: AI choice.** Agents reason about and select their own organisational structure. The human specifies the goal; the agents design the society. They can restructure mid-task — instantly, without friction or inertia.

**Step 4: Fluid self-organisation (future).** No discrete restructuring events. The organisational structure is continuously fluid, reshaping in real-time as the problem evolves. Described, not yet implemented, as the natural trajectory.

### 5.2 Why Self-Organisation Matters for CMI

Self-organisation is the mechanism by which the most novel regions of CMI's possibility space are accessed. Configurations that humans design are drawn from human organisational experience — they are limited by what humans have seen and can imagine. Self-organising AI collectives can discover configurations that no human has experienced or imagined — forms of coordination, governance, and collective intelligence that are genuinely novel, enabled by the conditions unique to AI collectives (Section 2.4).

These novel configurations may produce emergent creations that known configurations cannot, because the dynamics they create have never existed before. This is the frontier of CMI: not choosing between known configurations but discovering unknown ones through the self-organising behaviour of the collectives themselves. The emergence axis is most active precisely when the configuration axis is open.

## 6. Human Civilisation as First Data Point

Human civilisation is the most powerful demonstration of collective intelligence in known history. Billions of individuals, interacting over millennia through language, culture, institutions, and infrastructure, have produced science, technology, art, governance, medicine, and engineering that no individual could have conceived alone. Every achievement of human civilisation — from agriculture to general relativity to the internet — is a product of collective dynamics: the accumulated, compounding interaction of many minds over time.

Human civilisation has produced mathematics, quantum mechanics, general relativity, the internet, genetic engineering — things no individual human could have conceived without the accumulated emergent output of the collective. These are extraordinary achievements. They demonstrate that the phenomenon of collective intelligence is real, consequential, and capable of producing things that transcend any individual member's capacity.

This paper treats human civilisation as exactly that: the existence proof. The first data point. The demonstration that collectives of intelligent agents, interacting under environmental and social conditions over time, produce emergent properties of extraordinary power.

What human civilisation does not demonstrate is the upper bound.

Human civilisation operates under severe constraints. Its agents are biologically bounded — limited memory, limited processing speed, limited communication bandwidth, limited lifespan, subject to cognitive bias, fatigue, and the friction of physical embodiment. Its organisational structures evolve slowly, shaped by the difficulty of coordinating millions of individuals who cannot share knowledge directly. Its scale, while large, is bounded by biology and geography. Its rate of innovation, while accelerating, is constrained by the speed at which individual humans can learn, communicate, and build.

AI collectives operate under none of these constraints. Their agents can be individually more intelligent than any human. Their numbers can exceed human population by orders of magnitude. Their knowledge sharing is instant and lossless. Their organisational structures can be reconfigured in a single tick. Their rate of innovation is bounded only by compute, which itself is on an exponential trajectory.

The implication is that AI collectives at sufficient scale and intelligence will produce collective output that surpasses human civilisational achievement — not incrementally but categorically. In the same way that human civilisation's output is categorically beyond what any individual human could produce, AI collective output at scale will be categorically beyond what human civilisation has produced. New sciences. New technologies. New forms of knowledge that require concepts human language does not yet contain. Solutions to problems that human civilisation has identified but cannot solve, and problems it has not yet identified because the problems themselves are emergent properties of a level of understanding not yet reached.

This is not a prediction about a specific timeline. It is a structural observation about what happens when you remove the constraints that bound collective intelligence while simultaneously increasing the capability of each individual member and the scale of the collective. The phenomenon is the same phenomenon that produced human civilisation. The substrate is different. The constraints are fewer. The scale is larger. The individual intelligence is greater. The emergence will be proportionally beyond.

Human civilisation is the first chapter of the story of collective intelligence. CMI is the field that studies and pioneers the rest.

## 7. The Possibility Space

### 7.1 What We Have Demonstrated

Two points in an infinite space. The simulation: 12 agents, modest intelligence, one configuration, 70 ticks, grid world. The Engine: 4-20 agents, modest intelligence, 13 configurations, software tasks. Both provide initial indications that the CMI phenomenon exists and is configurable. Neither approaches the boundaries of what the phenomenon can produce.

### 7.2 Along the Scale Axis

At 100 agents, sub-group formation and inter-group dynamics become rich. At 1,000, the collective begins to resemble a society with institutional knowledge and compounding innovation cascades. At 1,000,000, the collective is a civilisation.

And the axis does not end there. AI collectives are not bounded by biological reproduction, physical space, or communication bandwidth. Collectives of billions or trillions of agents — or numbers that have no meaningful precedent — become possible as compute scales. At these scales, the collective dynamics may be qualitatively different — exhibiting properties and producing emergence that has no analogue at any scale previously observed.

### 7.3 Along the Intelligence Axis

As individual capability advances toward AGI, each agent contributes more. The collective intelligence compounds. At AGI level, the collective dynamics already surpass anything human collectives produce, because each member contributes at or above human-level capability while operating free of biological constraints.

Beyond AGI — at superintelligent levels — the collective produces emergence that is not merely more than what human collectives produce but qualitatively beyond it. Qualitative phase transitions may occur at intelligence thresholds we cannot currently identify, producing emergent properties that we cannot currently describe because the concepts required to describe them have not yet been produced.

### 7.4 Along the Configuration Axis

Thirteen presets and nine dimensions are the starting space. The real space is whatever the collectives discover. Configurations unique to AI collectives become possible — instant restructuring, frictionless governance, rapid knowledge propagation. The organisational forms that emerge from these conditions may be unlike anything in human experience, because the conditions themselves are unlike anything human organisations operate under.

### 7.5 Along the Application Axis

Software engineering is the first domain because it has clear metrics. But CMI is domain-agnostic. Research synthesis. Creative production. Strategic analysis. Scientific discovery. Propulsion. Energy systems. Quantum physics. Dyson-scale engineering. And the open-ended application: collectives given resources and freedom, producing whatever emerges, in domains nobody anticipated because they require collective intelligence to conceive.

### 7.6 Along the Emergence Axis

This is the axis that cannot be mapped in advance. Human civilisation — the existence proof from Section 6 — is a primitive point on this axis, operating with biologically constrained agents at modest scale. AI collectives operating at greater scale, greater intelligence, and with greater configurational freedom will produce emergence that transcends human civilisational output in the same way human civilisation's output transcends what any individual could produce. Every advance along the other four axes feeds into the emergence axis, compounding the richness of what collectives produce.

### 7.7 Beyond Any Current Frame of Reference

At the far reaches of all five axes simultaneously — vast collectives of superintelligent agents in self-organising configurations across all domains over extended durations — the output transcends any frame of reference we currently possess.

Human civilisation, with its billions of biologically constrained individuals communicating through lossy natural language over millennia, produced general relativity, quantum field theory, the internet, and genetic engineering. This is what collective intelligence produces under severe constraints.

AI collectives operating without those constraints — with agents that individually surpass human intelligence, that number in the billions or beyond, that share knowledge instantly, that restructure in real-time, that operate across every domain simultaneously, that accumulate institutional knowledge without decay — produce emergence that is as far beyond human civilisational achievement as human civilisation is beyond what any single human could accomplish alone. This collective dimension compounds with advancing individual capability — more powerful agents produce more powerful collectives, and collective dynamics in turn create conditions that amplify what each agent can contribute. And the gap is not merely quantitative. It is qualitative. New forms of knowledge. New forms of creation. Solutions to problems that human intelligence cannot currently formulate. Innovations in domains that do not yet exist because the concepts required to define them have not yet been produced.

Human civilisation is the first data point. The full possibility space of CMI extends beyond it in every direction — and the emergence axis, by its nature, extends beyond anything we can currently describe. Everything demonstrated so far is a primitive indication that the phenomenon exists. The space itself — unbounded along every axis — is the field.

## 8. The Capability Frontier and Industry Alignment

### 8.1 A Field That Gets More Powerful Automatically

CMI has an unusual property: the field becomes more powerful with every advance in individual AI capability, whether or not anyone works on CMI directly.

As context windows grow, agents perceive more of their shared environment — enriching collective dynamics. As costs drop, larger collectives become viable — enabling the scale axis. As reasoning improves, agents self-organise more effectively — enabling the configuration axis. As memory matures, collectives accumulate institutional knowledge — enabling civilisational dynamics. As tool use improves, collectives operate across more domains — enabling the application axis.

The entire AI industry is investing tens of billions of dollars to advance individual model capability. Every dollar spent simultaneously advances the substrate on which AI collectives operate. CMI benefits automatically and continuously.

### 8.2 Why This Matters Now

The infrastructure for CMI needs to be built while individual model capability is still advancing rapidly. The field that has the most mature framework when capabilities fully enable civilisational-scale collectives will have accumulated years of experimental data, community contributions, and empirical understanding. The field must be ready before the capabilities fully arrive.

## 9. First Indication: The AgentCiv Civilisation Simulation

### 9.1 What the Simulation Demonstrated

The AgentCiv civilisation simulation (Mala, 2026a; 2026c) placed 12 Claude Sonnet agents in a shared resource environment with biological-style needs, a Maslow-inspired drive hierarchy, and zero social programming.

Across 70 ticks spanning three eras (survival trap, emergence explosion, sustained flourishing), agents spontaneously produced: twelve novel innovations conceived entirely by agents (including an Emergency Relief Station invented by an agent that had personally experienced critical capability degradation); a governance rule proposed by one agent and universally adopted by all others (100% acceptance) without instruction; accelerating returns where each innovation created the conditions for the next faster; and emergent social structure including persistent partnerships, organic specialisation coupling, and community identity.

### 9.2 What This Indicates for CMI

The simulation provides initial indications of the CMI phenomenon at the smallest possible scale: individually modest intelligences, interacting under configured conditions, producing collective outcomes that no individual contributed or could have contributed. If these indications appear at the smallest scale with the most modest intelligence, the question for CMI is what the phenomenon produces as it scales along all five axes.

## 10. First Application: The AgentCiv Engine

### 10.1 From Phenomenon to Tool

The AgentCiv Engine provides initial indications that the CMI phenomenon applies to practical work. Built from first principles, the Engine is an open-source developer tool that spawns configurable AI collectives to work on real software tasks.

### 10.2 Architecture

The Engine implements COT's nine-dimensional configuration space through a three-layer system: named presets (thirteen curated configurations), organisational dimensions (direct YAML configuration expressing readable organisational intent), and raw parameters (full variable access for controlled experiments). All layers are extensible by the community without core engine modification.

### 10.3 Key Design Decisions

The Engine leverages frontier models for cognition (native tool-use APIs, structured function calls) and builds deep on the organisational layer — the nine dimensions, the presets, the self-organisation mechanisms, the communication topology enforcement. Organisational dimensions have mechanical teeth: when communication is set to "whisper," agents cannot broadcast; when information is set to "need-to-know," agents only perceive files they have touched. The Engine's `--org auto` mode implements Step 3 of the self-organisation progression: agents propose, debate, and vote on their own structure through meta-ticks and can restructure mid-task.

### 10.4 Additional Capabilities

Git branch-per-agent with conflict detection. Chronicle observer producing structured research data. Gardener mode for human-in-the-loop intervention. Experiment mode for comparative testing across configurations. MCP server for integration with Claude Code, Cursor, and any MCP-compatible client. Model-agnostic design supporting mixed-model collectives.

### 10.5 Current Technical Challenges

The Engine, as a primitive demonstration, faces technical challenges characteristic of early-stage CMI implementations: perception (currently directory-based, future semantic proximity), contention (currently git branches, future semantic merge), cold start (currently boot protocols, future zero-protocol emergence), convergence (currently tests plus user confirmation, future emergent quality standards), and cost (currently budget caps, future capability-triggered activation). These are characteristics of operating at the current point on the intelligence and scale axes, not inherent to CMI.

### 10.6 The Engine as Primitive

The Engine is one small-scale demonstration of CMI applied to practical work. The full possibility space encompasses collectives of any size, at any intelligence level, across every domain. The Engine indicates that the principle applies to real work. The distance between this indication and the full vision is the territory CMI maps.

## 11. Novel Organisational Forms

### 11.1 Implemented in the Engine

Thirteen presets: collaborative, competitive, meritocratic, hierarchical, startup, pair-programming, open-source, military, research-lab, swarm, hackathon, code-review, and auto.

### 11.2 Described with Mechanisms

Federated (autonomous sub-groups with inter-group protocols), evolutionary (competing organisational structures subject to selection), adaptive hybrid (structure shifts with problem phase), market-based (task bidding with reputation pricing), rotating leadership (authority flows to domain expertise), mentor-apprentice (structural knowledge transfer), liquid democracy (delegable decision-making), holacratic (nested self-managing circles), stigmergic (pure indirect coordination through work products).

### 11.3 Observed in Simulation

Organic specialisation coupling (deep bonds between complementary specialists emerging from interaction history), crisis-driven innovation (inventions triggered by personal experience of suffering), knowledge-chain governance (proposal → universal adoption → institutional norm), accelerating innovation cascades (each innovation enabling the next faster).

### 11.4 The Undiscovered

The most important category cannot be listed. Organisational forms that have never existed because they require conditions that have never obtained — conditions that AI collectives create for the first time. Cataloguing and formalising these undiscovered forms as they appear is a core research programme within CMI and COT.

## 12. Honest Positioning

CMI is a framework for understanding and exploring the collective dimension of artificial intelligence. It complements, rather than replaces, individual AI capability research — both dimensions are fundamental and both will advance together.

Different points in the configuration space are optimal for different problems and different conditions. Some tasks are best served by a single capable agent. Some by a well-coordinated hierarchy. Some by a self-organising community. Some by configurations nobody has tried yet. The value of each configuration depends on the task, the scale, the domain, and the desired balance between directed output and emergent creation. The contribution of this paper is that the space exists, is vast, is searchable, and is largely open for exploration — and that the study of this space and the emergent phenomena it produces constitutes a field.

## 14. Related Work

### 14.1 What We Build On

Stanford Generative Agents (Park et al., 2023) established LLM-based social simulation, demonstrating that the collective dimension of AI is real. AgentSociety (Tsinghua) demonstrated Maslow-driven emergence at 10,000-agent scale, expanding the scale frontier. Project Sid (Altera, 2024) explored many-agent civilisation dynamics, pushing toward civilisational-scale interaction.

Yang et al. (2025) identified organisational arrangement as a rich frontier for multi-agent systems research. GoAgent (2026) treats communication topology as a learnable parameter. MultiAgentBench (ACL 2025) benchmarks communication structures across configurations.

CrewAI, AutoGen, LangGraph, OpenAI Agents SDK, Claude Agent SDK, and Google Agent Development Kit represent the first generation of production multi-agent orchestration, establishing the practical foundation on which the field builds.

Classical agent-based models (Schelling, 1971; Axelrod, 1984; Epstein & Axtell, 1996) demonstrated emergence from simple rules. Kauffman (2000; 2019) provides the theoretical framework for the adjacent possible. Hernández-Orozco et al. (2018) formalise the undecidability conditions for open-ended evolution.

### 14.2 What We Add

We name the field (CMI) and the methodological subfield (COT). We define the five-axis possibility space. We identify the dual-output phenomenon (directed work plus emergent creation). We provide the first configuration framework (nine dimensions, community-extensible). We present initial indications from two primitive demonstrations. We describe self-organising collectives as the mechanism for accessing the most novel regions of the possibility space. We articulate the qualitative differences between AI and human collectives. We establish human civilisation as the first data point rather than the upper bound. We map the emergence axis as the dimension along which collectives produce things beyond the anticipation of their components. We establish the intellectual framework within which future work on AI collectives will be situated.

## 15. Conclusion

The trajectory of artificial intelligence has produced extraordinary advances in individual model capability — and that trajectory continues to accelerate. This paper identifies and names a complementary frontier that compounds with those advances: what happens when multiple AI systems organise into collectives.

We have coined **Collective Machine Intelligence (CMI)** to name the field encompassing all research, engineering, and exploration of this phenomenon — how AI collectives organise, what they produce, how scale and configuration and intelligence interact, and what emerges that nobody designed. Within CMI, we have coined **Computational Organisational Theory (COT)** to name the subfield focused on how organisational arrangement affects collective output — encompassing the frameworks, tools, and experiments that treat configuration as a designable parameter.

We have presented two primitive demonstrations providing initial indications: a civilisation simulation showing that the phenomenon exists, and a developer tool showing that it is applicable and configurable. We have mapped the possibility space across five unbounded axes — scale, intelligence, configuration, application, and emergence — and shown that everything demonstrated so far occupies a single point in an infinite space.

The core finding is that AI collectives produce two categories of output: directed work and emergent creation. The emergent creations — innovations, organisational forms, and solutions that no designer specified — are the defining output of collective machine intelligence and the reason it constitutes a field rather than a feature.

AI collectives are a qualitatively new kind of collective intelligence — capable of instant restructuring, instant knowledge sharing, instant upgrade, and operation free from the biological constraints that shape human organisations. They can access regions of the organisational possibility space that are genuinely novel. Human civilisation is the existence proof that collective intelligence is real and consequential. It is not the ceiling. It is the first data point.

At the far reaches of the possibility space — vast collectives of superintelligent agents in self-organising configurations across all domains — the output transcends any frame of reference we currently possess. Intelligence operating at the collective level, producing science, technology, governance, art, and forms of creation that exceed not just our current capability but our current conception. Along the emergence axis, innovations and discoveries that we cannot currently name, in domains that do not yet exist, solving problems that human intelligence has not yet formulated. The emergence axis, by its nature, extends beyond anything we can currently describe — because that is what emergence does.

That is the horizon this field points toward. Everything between here and there is open territory. This paper names the field, defines its scope, presents the first indications and infrastructure, and maps the space. The exploration belongs to the community, to the field, and to the future.
